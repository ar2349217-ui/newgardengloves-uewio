Garden Gloves

1.  Leather Gloves

    *   Good for: Tough jobs like trimming roses or cacti, dealing with rough stuff, and just general gardening.
    *   What's cool: They last forever, protect you from getting poked, and keep your hands safe.
    *   What's not: Your hands might get sweaty when it's hot.
    *   How to clean: Use linseed oil or saddle soap to keep them soft and waterproof.
2.  Cotton Gloves

    *   Good for: Easy stuff like planting flowers, pulling weeds, and potting plants.
    *   What's cool: They let your hands breathe, feel nice, and don't weigh you down.
    *   What's not: They won't stop thorns or water.
    *   How to clean: Just throw them in the washing machine and let them air dry.
3.  Rubber or Latex-Coated Gloves

    *   Good for: When you're working with water, plant food, or yucky chemicals.
    *   What's cool: They don't let water in, they're tough, and they help you grip stuff.
    *   What's not: Your hands might get sweaty if you wear them too long.
    *   How to clean: Wash them with soap and water, but don't use anything strong or put them near heat.
4.  Synthetic Gloves (Like Nitrile or Nylon)

    *   Good for: Doing most gardening stuff where you need to be able to move your fingers.
    *   What's cool: They're light, stretchy, and don't tear easily.
    *   What's not: Leather gloves are better if you need to be safe from sharp things.
    *   How to clean: You can wash them in the machine, then let them air dry.
5.  Gauntlet Gloves

    *   Good for: Cutting thorny stuff and handling things that can hurt you.
    *   What's cool: They're long, so they protect your wrists and arms.
    *   What's not: They can make it harder to move your hands because they cover so much.
    *   How to clean: Just clean them based on what they're made of. For example, use leather cleaner for leather gloves, or wash synthetic gloves.

Here's a quick chart:

| Material        | Good For                 | What's Cool                | What's Not                  |
| --------------- | ------------------------ | -------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Leather         | Heavy jobs               | Tough, protects you        | Not very breathable           |
| Cotton          | Light jobs                 | Breathable, comfy           | Doesn't protect much        |
| Rubber/Coated   | Water/chemicals          | Waterproof, good grip      | Not very breathable           |
| Synthetic       | Most jobs, need fingers | Light, moves with you      | Doesn't stop sharp stuff as much |
| Gauntlet        | Thorns, dangerous stuff | Protects arms               | Harder to move your hands     |

How to make your gloves last:

*   Leather Gloves: Brush off dirt, clean with saddle soap, and use linseed oil to keep them soft.
*   Cotton Gloves: Wash them in the machine and let them air dry.
*   Rubber/Coated Gloves: Use soap and water, but don't use strong cleaners or heat.
*   Synthetic Gloves: Wash them and let them dry, but don't use high heat.

Some ideas for picking the right gloves:

*   What are you doing? Think about the job you're doing so you can grab the right type of gloves.
*   Do they fit right? Make sure they fit without being too tight. If they're comfy, you'll be happier.
*   What do you need to be safe from? Pick gloves that will protect you from thorns, chemicals, or water, depending on what you're doing.

Make sure to clean your gloves so they last.
​6. Waterproof Gloves

These are usually rubber or made of that wet suit stuff, neoprene.

Great when things are wet or muddy, like when you're messing with sprinklers or after it rains.

They stop your skin from getting all soggy and keep the dirt off.

Find some with comfy insides, so your hands don't get too sweaty.

7. Heat-Proof Gloves

Made from stuff that can take the heat, like Kevlar or special leather.

Good if you're near fire, grills, or messing with hot soil.

They also protect you from the sun when you're out there for hours.

8. Cut-Proof Gloves

They use stuff like Kevlar or steel mesh inside.

Perfect when you're doing some major trimming, using pointy stuff, or dealing with thorny plants.

They keep you from getting hurt, but you might not be as good at using your hands.

🌱 Special Gardening Gloves

For Seeds: Thin and lets your skin breathe, so you can feel the dirt and seeds.

For Roses: Longer and thicker, made of leather or tough stuff, so thorns don't scratch you up.

For Chemicals: Made of nitrile or PVC. They keep the bad stuff in pesticides and fertilizer off your skin.

For Cold Days: Warm and keep sweat away when you're gardening in winter.

⚠️ Good Ideas When You're Using Gardening Gloves

Check 'Em: Look for holes before you start, so you don't get hurt.

Get the Right Size: If they're too big, they'll get caught on stuff. Too small, and your hands will get tired.

Use the Right Gloves: Don't use thick gloves for things where you need to be able to feel well. Thin gloves won't keep you safe when you're doing heavy trimming.

Watch Out for Allergies: Some people are allergic to latex. If so, get some made of nitrile or leather.

🛠️ Keeping Them Nice

Dry Them Out: Dry your gloves after you wash them or use them when it's wet, so they don't get moldy.

Switch Them Out: Have some different pairs for different jobs, and use them at different times, so they last longer.

Put Them Away Right: Keep them where it's cool and dry. Keep them out of the sun and away from chemicals.

Fix Small Rips: You can patch up some leather gloves to make them last longer.

👋 Good Stuff About Wearing Gardening Gloves

Keeps you from getting cut, scraped, and poked by thorns.

Keeps your hands clean and stops soil germs from getting in.

Lowers the chance of your skin getting irritated or having a bad reaction.

Helps you hold onto tools and plants better.

Stops blisters when you're doing the same thing over and over, like digging or pulling weeds.
